Handicapping

Handicapping in netGolfLeague

NGL's handicap system levels the playing field so golfers of all abilities can compete fairly. Here's everything you need to know.

Overview

How NGL Handicapping Works

netGolfLeague uses a handicap system based on the USGA methodology. Handicaps are calculated automatically from the scores your golfers post each week — no manual math required.

NGL uses handicaps 1–9 (not 1–18) to accommodate leagues that play nine holes and courses with varying configurations, including 36-hole facilities.

Three Supported Handicap Methods

  • Standard — no stroke limit applied
  • Standard with ESC (Equitable Stroke Control) — adjusts unusually high hole scores before calculating the differential
  • Standard with Stroke Average — uses a stroke average approach rather than differentials

Choose your method in Setup → League and Season Options → Handicap Method.

Concepts

What is a Handicap Index?

A Handicap Index is a decimal number that represents a golfer's demonstrated ability relative to par — independent of the specific course they're playing. It's a portable measure of skill that works on any rated course.

Example: a golfer with a Handicap Index of 12.4 typically shoots about 12–13 strokes over par on a course of average difficulty.

How it's calculated

NGL uses the most recent 20 scores and seeds to calculate the Handicap Index. The system takes the best differentials from those rounds, averages them, and applies a multiplier to produce the final index.

Handicap Index vs. Course Handicap: The Handicap Index is your portable ability number. The Course Handicap is what you actually use on the scorecard — it's adjusted for the specific course's slope rating.
Concepts

What is a Course Handicap?

A Course Handicap is a whole number that tells you how many strokes you need to match a scratch golfer's performance on a specific course from a specific set of tees.

The Formula

Course Handicap = Handicap Index × (Slope Rating ÷ 113)

Where 113 is the standard slope rating for a course of average difficulty. A course with a higher slope is harder than average; a lower slope is easier.

Example: Handicap Index of 12.4 on a course with Slope Rating 125 = 14 Course Handicap.

Setup

Setting Initial (Seed) Handicaps

At the start of a new season, golfers don't yet have scores in the system. Seed handicaps give NGL a starting point for calculations.

1

Open Each Golfer's Profile

Navigate to Golfer Management and select a golfer to edit their profile.

2

Add Seed Scores

Enter historical scores along with the course slope and rating for each score. NGL incorporates these into the handicap calculation.

3

Save and Repeat

Repeat for all golfers. Seed scores appear alongside real scores in the 20-round calculation window.

Quick tip for Week 1: If you don't have historical scores, a simple starting handicap is each golfer's first week score minus par. Enter this as their seed so no one defaults to scratch (0 handicap).
Methods

Handicap Calculation Methods

Standard (No Stroke Limit)

Scores are used as-is to calculate the differential. No maximum is applied to any individual hole score. Best for informal leagues.

Standard with ESC (Equitable Stroke Control)

ESC caps the maximum score a golfer can record on any single hole before the differential is calculated. This prevents one bad hole from wildly skewing a handicap.

  • Course Handicap 0–9 → max score per hole: double bogey
  • Course Handicap 10–19 → max score per hole: 7
  • Course Handicap 20–29 → max score per hole: 8
  • Course Handicap 30–39 → max score per hole: 9
  • Course Handicap 40+ → max score per hole: 10

Standard with Stroke Average

Uses the average of the golfer's actual strokes per round rather than score differentials. Good for leagues that want a simpler, more intuitive handicap number.

Reference

Maximum Nine-Hole Handicap

The USGA sets maximum Handicap Index limits:

  • Men: 18.2
  • Women: 20.2

Note that the resulting Course Handicap may be higher than these values on courses with slope ratings above 113, since the slope multiplier increases the number.

Reference

How Course Ratings Are Established

Course ratings are conducted by the NCGA (or your regional golf association) every six years using trained volunteer raters. Raters evaluate 10 obstacles per hole to arrive at the official Course Rating and Slope Rating.

You can look up official course ratings at the USGA course rating database: ncrdb.usga.org

Course not in NGL's database? When setting up your schedule, use the option to submit a new course. You'll need the official course rating and slope from the scorecard or the USGA database.
